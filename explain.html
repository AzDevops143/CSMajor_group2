SNOOPFINGER Reimplementation and Evaluation

A brutal, honest security review of the gaze-snooping threat model paired with a live, click-to-type virtual keyboard sandbox.

Overview and Threat Model

The SNOOPFINGER side-channel attack targets virtual keyboards on head-mounted displays (HMDs). By harvesting orientation data (such as head-motion tracks and gaze orientation vectors) delivered to background or unprivileged applications, an adversary can reconstruct what word the user is typing on the keyboard.

While the original paper proposed several defense strategies in prose under "Adaptive Sensor Data Obfuscation", it failed to provide empirical evaluation metrics or target thresholds for these defenses. This reimplementation provides a quantitative framework to sweep, evaluate, and compare these defenses against the reconstructed attack mechanism.

Gaps and Limitations (Brutal and Honest Review)

A rigorous look at the paper reveals several theoretical over-simplifications and structural flaws. Below is a critical security assessment of the SNOOPFINGER threat model and defense boundaries:

Unrealistic Gaussian Noise Model
The simulation framework collapses orientation errors into independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) Gaussian coordinates. In real head/eye tracking, physical drift, postural tremors, and muscle fatigue cause highly correlated, non-i.i.d. noise over time. This flaw explains why the baseline simulation's accuracy increases with word length (the Central Limit Theorem averages out i.i.d. noise), while real-world user studies report decreasing accuracy.
Idealized 2D Keyboard Projection
The model assumes a flat, normalized 2D standard QWERTY grid. In deployed HMD platforms, virtual keyboards are projected onto curved 3D surfaces. Head rotation, relative distance, and perspective distortion introduce complex coordinate projections. By ignoring spatial perspective shifts, the threat model underestimates the normalization complexity required for a successful real-world attack.
Lack of Utility Metrics
The defense evaluation uses Root-Mean-Squared Error (RMSE) coordinate distortion as a proxy for utility loss. However, it fails to quantify usability impacts on legitimate HMD applications. In practice, injecting noise or quantization to protect typing privacy may degrade gaze-control accuracy, introduce rendering jitter, or cause user nausea, rendering the HMD unusable.
Keystroke Boundary Resolution
The attacker-side geometry matching assumes the dictionary length of the typed word is resolved perfectly, meaning the boundaries between key strikes are cleanly demarcated. Real orientation stream processing requires resolving noisy tap events. Missing or incorrectly segmenting a single tap boundary breaks the candidate length matching and ruins the inference.

Mathematical Formulation

Let each key center coordinate on the layout be designated as:

\[ K_c = (x_c, y_c) \]

The gaze trace coordinates corresponding to typing a word are simulated with additive Gaussian noise representing the sensor's baseline tracking inaccuracies:

\[ P_i = K_{w_i} + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) \]

Under a coordinate quantization defense of step size \(q\), the simulated gaze points are rounded to decrease orientation precision:

\[ P'_i = \text{round}\left(\frac{P_i}{q}\right) \times q \]

For any path, the consecutive geometry vectors \(\vec{v}_i\) yield the distance \(d_i\) and circular angle \(\theta_i\) signatures:

\[ \vec{v}_i = P_{i+1} - P_i \implies d_i = \|\vec{v}_i\|_2, \quad \theta_i = \text{arctan2}(\Delta y_i, \Delta x_i) \]

To evaluate a candidate word, we rank it by calculating the sum of squared deviations across consecutive distance metrics and wrapped angle differences:

\[ S(w_{cand}) = \sum_{i=1}^{L} \left( (d_{cand, i} - d_{obs, i})^2 + \left(\frac{\Delta\theta_i}{180}\right)^2 \right) \]

where \(\Delta\theta_i\) represents the smallest angular difference:

\[ \Delta\theta_i = (\theta_{cand, i} - \theta_{obs, i} + 180)^\circ \pmod{360} - 180^\circ \]

Evaluation and Defense Findings

By evaluating the SNOOPFINGER pipeline across parameter sweeps, we established the first concrete numbers for the proposed countermeasures.

**Conclusion**: Noise-based obfuscation is significantly more effective and predictable than coordinate quantization. While noise injection monotonically degrades the attacker's accuracy toward a random regime, coordinate quantization only degrades the attack when the rounding step is large enough to exceed the physical spacing between keyboard keys.
